# Jason Haddix

## Status

Draft v1 — person / ecosystem profile.

## How I discovered him

I discovered Jason Haddix through interviews, conference material and discussions around offensive security, bug bounty methodology, AI red teaming and modern attacker mindset.

He became especially interesting to me because he connects several worlds at once: practical offensive security, bug bounty, security leadership, training, AI-enabled systems and community building.

## Why he matters in my cyber culture

Jason Haddix matters because his work shows how offensive methodology can become structured, teachable and useful to defenders.

What I find interesting is not only the “hacker” side, but the way he frames offensive security as a repeatable method: recon, attack surface understanding, web application analysis, prioritization and communication with security leaders.

In the AI Security space, his work is also relevant because he approaches AI-enabled systems from a practical assessment perspective rather than only from abstract model safety. That connects directly to my interest in CTI and AI Security: the question is not only “can the model be jailbroken?”, but “what can an attacker do through the system, tools, data and workflow around the model?”

## Area

## What I learned from following his work

- Offensive security becomes more valuable when it is structured into repeatable methodology.
- Recon and attack surface understanding are central skills, not boring preparation.
- AI Security should focus on real systems, not only standalone model prompts.
- A strong security profile can combine hacker mindset, leadership experience and public education.
- Red team thinking is defensively useful when it helps organizations identify realistic paths of compromise.

## Relevance for CTI

For CTI (Cyber Threat Intelligence), Jason Haddix is useful because his work helps bridge attacker thinking and defensive prioritization.

A CTI analyst needs to understand how attackers chain weak signals into real compromise paths. Haddix’s focus on recon, web application testing and attack surface management is relevant because CTI is not only about naming malware families or groups. It is also about understanding what exposure an attacker would actually see and how they would operationalize it.

In the AI Security context, his work is relevant because agentic systems, AI-enabled applications and tool-connected models create new attack surfaces. A CTI analyst tracking AI threats needs to understand both classic offensive methodology and AI-specific abuse paths.

## Caveats

This profile should avoid treating Jason Haddix as a generic “influencer”. The useful lesson is methodology: how offensive security knowledge is organized, taught and translated into defensive action.

I should also separate company marketing, course descriptions, public talks and independently verifiable technical material. Training pages are useful to understand positioning, but they are not the same as primary technical evidence.
