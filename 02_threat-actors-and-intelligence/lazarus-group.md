# Lazarus Group

**Type:** state-linked threat actor / crypto theft / espionage

## Why it is on my radar

Lazarus is one of the first APT (Advanced Persistent Threat) names that made me understand the overlap between state objectives and cybercriminal methods. I encountered it through crypto hacks, WannaCry attribution discussions, the Ronin Bridge case and broader research on North Korean cyber operations.

## What stuck with me

What interests me is the hybrid nature of the actor. Lazarus is not just “a hacking group” in the simple sense. It sits at the intersection of espionage, financial theft, crypto targeting, sanctions evasion and geopolitics.

The crypto angle especially caught my attention because it shows that blockchain and exchanges are not only finance topics. They can become part of national security and strategic financing.

## What I take from it

Lazarus taught me that CTI (Cyber Threat Intelligence) cannot stay at the level of malware names or IOCs (Indicators of Compromise). You need to connect the actor, the target, the money movement, the social engineering and the political context.

It also reminds me to be careful with labels: “Lazarus” is often used as an umbrella term, and not every large crypto theft should automatically be attributed to them.

## Current limit

I still need to better understand the sub-clusters and naming differences between Lazarus, APT38, Bluenoroff and other DPRK-linked labels.
