# Lazarus and crypto operations

**Type:** DPRK-linked crypto theft / laundering / strategic financing

## Why it is on my radar

Lazarus crypto operations are one of the topics that best connect my interest in crypto, OSINT (Open Source Intelligence), CTI (Cyber Threat Intelligence) and geopolitics.

I became interested in this through Ronin, Bybit, ZachXBT, DPRK attribution stories, fake interview cases and the recurring role of mixers, bridges and laundering infrastructure.

## What stuck with me

What struck me is that these operations should not be read as ordinary cybercrime only. Public attribution often frames DPRK-linked crypto theft as a way to generate revenue for the North Korean regime.

The fake interview / remote worker angle also stayed with me because it shows that crypto companies can be approached through people and hiring processes, not only through code or wallets.

That changes the way I look at crypto hacks. The money trail is not just post-incident noise; it becomes part of the intelligence picture.

## What I take from it

This topic helped me understand why CTI needs to connect multiple layers: initial access, social engineering, wallet compromise, blockchain movement, sanctions, actor labels, remote-work abuse and geopolitical incentives.

It also reminds me to stay cautious: not every large crypto hack is Lazarus, and blockchain tracing alone is not enough to prove identity.

## Current limit

I still need to better distinguish the different DPRK-linked labels used by vendors and governments: Lazarus, APT38, TraderTraitor, Bluenoroff and others.
